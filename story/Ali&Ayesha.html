The Boy Who Lived Twice A story of silence, magic eyes, and letting go

Ali was the kind of boy who lived twice. Once in the real world, and once in his head.

In the real world, he was quiet, respectful, the sort of cousin elders liked because he never talked back. He studied, helped at home, prayed on Fridays, listened more than he spoke. In the village, people nodded when he passed. “Achha larka hai,” they said.

In his head, though, Ali lived entire lifetimes. That world had music playing in the background. Lana Del Rey on some nights, Michael Jackson on others. Scenes repeated themselves there, polished and perfected. Conversations went the right way. Smiles lingered longer. Outcomes bent in his favor. Ali told himself this was a gift. He could predict things. Feel them before they happened.

At the center of both worlds stood Ayesha. Ayesha was his cousin. Not too close, not too distant. Close enough for family gatherings, far enough for hope to sneak in. She lived almost a hundred kilometers away, far enough that longing had room to grow. She was calm, guarded, practical. When she spoke, she spoke lightly, like someone who did not want to leave footprints.

Ali could spend hours imagining her lips—the way they curved when she smiled, the way they moved when she spoke softly, as if every word carried a secret meant only for him.

He imagined running his fingers through her hair, though he knew he never would, memorizing the way it fell across her shoulders like a quiet secret. Her hands were among the most beautiful creations of God, and Ali’s longing to touch them burned so fiercely he would have given anything just for a single touch of her fingers.

Years ago, when Ali was younger and dumber, he sent her a message in Arabic. “I love you.” He thought it was poetic. Grown up. Brave. Ayesha was shocked. A little angry. The silence that followed taught him his first lesson, though he did not fully understand it then.

Time passed. Ali grew. Or at least, he thought he did. He watched her life from a distance. Occasional chats. Long gaps. One time she blocked him. Six months of silence. He told himself stories about courage and fear, about how girls sometimes block the men they secretly like. The stories made the waiting easier. They also made the attachment stronger.

Ali was a big fan of Harry Potter movies, but the eyes of Ayesha had more magic than all seven movies combined.

He was so fond of her eyes that he wished, if not Ayesha, then a daughter with her eyes. Someone to call his own, someone to protect. Because he couldn’t have her, he tried to have her through someone else.

I’ll look for her in every lifetime.
I know you’ll never be mine, but still I close my eyes and imagine how beautiful it would be if we were together.

He had fallen in love with those eyes so completely that all others had become invisible. Nothing else mattered. Not friends. Not sights. Not life.

One night, years later, Ali sat in the village listening to Ayesha’s sister and her husband tell their love story. They laughed about how she had not liked him at first. How time changed things. How persistence, done “the right way,” won in the end.

Something clicked in Ali’s chest. “Why not me?” he thought.

That night, he sent a simple message.

Hey, how’ve you been?

Minutes passed. Then an hour. Then two.

Ali watched the clock the way people watch heart monitors. 21:10. 22:00. 23:27. After midnight. Nothing.

His head filled the silence for her. He imagined replies. Rejections. Confessions. He imagined proposing properly. He imagined going to parents. He imagined sacrificing ego, pride, everything. He told himself love meant endurance. He told himself stopping would kill him.

But there was another truth Ali could not escape. Ayesha had never chosen him. Not once in two years had she texted first. Not once had she crossed the distance he kept running across alone. When asked directly, she had said, “I’ll do whatever my father decides.” Not yes. Not no. A deflection wrapped in tradition.

Ali lay awake that night, realizing something painful.

Love is not proven by how much you are willing to suffer. Love is proven by whether the other person steps toward you.

By morning, Ali understood the real test was not whether Ayesha would say yes. The test was whether he would respect a no. Whether he would stop if stopping was required. Whether he would choose dignity over obsession. Whether he would let a story end instead of forcing it into tragedy.

Some people are meant to be loved from afar. Some lessons only arrive after a very long night. And some eyes, no matter how magical, are never yours to keep.
